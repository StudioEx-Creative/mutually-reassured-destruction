Chapter 01 · The Record

The planet keeps its own books. Every one of them is breaking.

These are the canonical indicators of a changing climate, each drawn from the agency that measures it. Tap any card for its full record, its method, and its source. Ember marks the carbon and the heat; ice marks the cryosphere and the ocean absorbing it.

The record, in rings · global temperature, 1850–2025

Each vertical stripe is one year, coloured by its global mean temperature relative to the 1850–1900 average — deep blue for the coolest years around 1900, through pale tones mid-century, to deep red for the record heat of the 2020s. The overall movement is a steady shift from blue to red, accelerating after 1970.

Each stripe is one year, coloured by its temperature against the 1850–1900 average. The visual concept is Ed Hawkins' #ShowYourStripes; data follows the Berkeley Earth annual record.

An ice core is drawn as a cylinder of layered ice holding bubbles of trapped air — the actual atmosphere, sealed in the year the snow fell. Depth reads as time, with now at the top. Beside it, the carbon dioxide the core carries is traced: a long flat line at about 280 parts per million for the air before us, then, above a declared scale break, the real instrumental record from 1958 climbing steeply to 430.5 parts per million in May 2025, the first month above 430.

One hundred squares represent all the extra heat trapped by greenhouse gases. They begin in the air. As the page scrolls they fall, and ninety of the hundred sink through the sea surface and down the water column past 700 and 2000 metres, where they remain. Only about ten stay near the top, shared between the land, the ice and the atmosphere. The ocean has absorbed roughly 90% of the heat we have trapped, so the warming felt in the air is what is left over.

The ocean has taken about 90% of the extra heat — so the warming we argue about is the remainder. Heat in the ocean is not heat gone: it raises sea level as the water expands, and it comes back as marine heatwaves. NOAA NCEI / Cheng et al. ↗

A ring is a year.

A stump is a record, closed.

From orbit, the cut has a shape.

A single set of concentric growth rings fills the frame — one tree, read from above, each ring a year. A cut passes through it. The view pulls back and the single stump multiplies into a field of stumps, the ground draining from green to ash. Pulling back further, the stumps resolve into the fishbone pattern of roads and side-cuts that clear-cutting leaves across the Amazon — the shape the loss makes when seen from orbit.

Methodology & disclaimer ▾
A work of data art and advocacy, not a policy document. The climate is a system of staggering complexity, and every projection here carries uncertainty that we have tried to surface, not hide. The point is not the precision of any single number; it is the direction of all of them taken together. We have not invented or inflated any figure; all data was verified against primary sources in June 2026. Where authoritative sources disagree, we use the most recent peer-reviewed value and note the disagreement. The remaining-carbon-budget figure — about four years for a 50% chance of 1.5 °C — is from the Global Carbon Budget 2025 remaining-budget table, and always carries that probability. Forecast values beyond a provider's published years (e.g. 2040) are linearly interpolated and labelled as such. Full sources, retrieval dates and refresh notes are in the project's DATA-SOURCES file.
The world is not simple.
But the arithmetic of carbon is, and so is the choice it leaves us.
